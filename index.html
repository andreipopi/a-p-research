Doctoral Thesis

Title

"Algorithms Utilizing Structure for Reasoning in Abstract, Structured, and Probabilistic Argumentation"

Thesis Overview

Argumentative reasoning is a core decision-making paradigm in AI; it exhibits several desirable features such as being exact, explainable, expressive, and well-suited to high-stakes real world application domains. A major barrier in argumentative reasoning is the computational complexity arising in several argumentation formalisms, under different semantics. In this work, we approach complexity by focusing on underlying structural properties of argumentation graphs (allowing fixed-parameter algorithms), and developing dynamic programming algorithms that scale in relation to such properties, in this instance, treewidth. We look at different formalisms under different semantics, including:

  • Assumption-Based Argumentation (ABA) Frameworks;
  • Probabilistic Abstract Argumentation Frameworks (PAF);
  • Bipolar Probabilistic Abstract Argumentation Frameworks (BAF).

We implement working prototypes that show promising performance. Towards facilitating knowledge engineering in argumentative reasoning, we look further into structured arguments in ABA, and develop a declarative approach for the hard problem of completing partial arguments. Our results encourage investigating the extension of our algorithms to other prominent semantics as future work, as well as investigating pre-processing techniques which can reduce the treewidth of argumentation frameworks, setting the stage to apply our algorithms. More generally, it is interesting to investigate to what degree the main ideas of our algorithms are adaptable to other parameters that allow parameterised tractability in argumentative reasoning.

Keywords

Computational argumentation; argumentative reasoning; tree-decomposition-based algorithms; treewidth; structure.
